Abstract

This paper presents a comprehensive introduction to quasiperiodic functions in multiple variables, designed for undergraduate students in mathematics and physics. We begin with the fundamental theory of quasiperiodicity, exploring the connection between irrational frequencies and dense orbits on tori. Through interactive visualizations embedded directly in this document, we demonstrate how geodesics on flat and embedded tori illustrate key concepts in quasiperiodic dynamics. We examine specific examples including smooth triply periodic functions and piecewise linear functions related to polyhedra families. The paper concludes with applications to quasicrystals and condensed matter physics, particularly the magnetoresistance of lead. Our approach emphasizes visual intuition while maintaining mathematical rigor, supported by interactive tools that allow real-time exploration of these fascinating mathematical objects.

1. Introduction

1.1 Historical Context

In 1982, materials scientist Dan Shechtman peered through his electron microscope at a rapidly cooled aluminum-manganese alloy and observed something that shouldn't exist: a crystal with five-fold symmetry. This discovery would challenge a fundamental tenet of crystallography—that crystals must have translational symmetry—and eventually earn Shechtman the 2011 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.

What Shechtman had discovered was a quasicrystal, a material whose atomic arrangement is ordered but never repeats, embodying in physical form the mathematical concept of quasiperiodicity that had been studied abstractly for decades.

1.2 Motivation

Why should undergraduate students study quasiperiodic functions? Three compelling reasons emerge:

Mathematical Beauty

Quasiperiodic functions exhibit a fascinating interplay between order and disorder. They provide concrete examples of ergodic theory, Diophantine approximation, and dynamical systems. The appearance of fundamental constants like the golden ratio φ in these patterns connects number theory to geometry in unexpected ways.

Physical Relevance

From quasicrystals to electronic properties of materials, quasiperiodic structures appear throughout physics and materials science. Applications include:

  • Non-stick cookware coatings
  • LED light technology
  • Surgical instruments
  • Photonic bandgap materials

2. Mathematical Foundations

2.1 Periodic vs. Quasiperiodic Functions

Definition 2.1: A function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is periodic if there exist linearly independent vectors $\mathbf{p}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{p}_n \in \mathbb{R}^n$ such that $$f(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{p}_i) = f(\mathbf{x}) \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n \text{ and } i = 1, \ldots, n.$$
Definition 2.2: A function $f: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ is quasiperiodic with $n$ frequencies if there exist:
  • A continuous function $F: \mathbb{T}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ on the $n$-dimensional torus
  • Frequency vectors $\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_n \in \mathbb{R}^d$ that are linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$
such that $$f(\mathbf{x}) = F(\omega_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}, \omega_2 \cdot \mathbf{x}, \ldots, \omega_n \cdot \mathbf{x})$$
