Abstract

This paper presents a comprehensive introduction to quasiperiodic functions in multiple variables, designed specifically for undergraduate students with minimal prerequisites. We begin with the intuitive concept of patterns that maintain structure without repetition, exemplified by Penrose tilings. Through interactive visualizations embedded directly in this document, readers can explore how simple mathematical rules create infinitely complex patterns. We carefully build from one-dimensional examples to the beautiful geometry of geodesics on tori. The paper culminates in understanding how these mathematical concepts explain real-world phenomena, from the atomic structure of quasicrystals to the electronic properties of materials. Our approach emphasizes hands-on exploration: every concept can be manipulated and visualized in real-time.

1. Introduction

1.1 What Are Quasiperiodic Functions?

Imagine listening to two musical notes played together. When the frequencies form a simple ratio like 2:1 (an octave), the combined sound repeats regularly: it's periodic. But what happens when the ratio is irrational, like π:1? The pattern maintains clear structure without ever repeating exactly. This is quasiperiodicity.

Everyday Example: Moiré Patterns

Have you ever seen two window screens overlapping, creating shifting patterns as you move? Or noticed strange patterns when taking a photo of a computer screen? These moiré effects are visual examples of quasiperiodicity: two regular patterns combining to create something that looks almost regular but isn't quite.

1.2 The Discovery That Changed Physics

For centuries, scientists believed crystals could only have certain symmetries. A square tile can cover a floor with 4-fold symmetry, hexagons give 6-fold symmetry, but 5-fold? Impossible, or so we thought.

On April 8, 1982, Dan Shechtman looked at an electron diffraction pattern of a rapidly cooled aluminum-manganese alloy and saw something "forbidden": sharp diffraction spots arranged with 5-fold symmetry. His notebook entry that day included the note "10 Fold???" reflecting his disbelief.

The Cost of Discovery

Shechtman's discovery was so controversial that Linus Pauling declared: "There is no such thing as quasicrystals, only quasi-scientists." Shechtman was even asked to leave his research group. But he persisted, and in 2011, he received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for discovering these materials with quasiperiodic atomic arrangements.

1.3 Why Should You Care?

Quasiperiodic functions aren't just mathematical curiosities. They appear in:

  • Your Kitchen: Some non-stick pans use quasicrystalline coatings because they're incredibly hard and have low friction
  • LED Lights: Photonic quasicrystals can control light in ways periodic structures cannot
  • Medical Devices: Quasicrystalline materials in surgical instruments stay sharper longer
  • Future Computing: Quasiperiodic structures might enable new types of quantum computers

1.4 Your Journey Through This Paper

We'll start with simple examples you can play with, gradually building your intuition. By the end, you'll understand:

  1. How adding waves with irrational frequency ratios creates never-repeating patterns
  2. Why walking on a torus at certain angles creates beautiful, space-filling curves
  3. How nature uses quasiperiodicity to create structures we're only beginning to understand

2. Mathematical Foundations

2.1 Starting Simple: What Does "Periodic" Mean?

Let's begin with something familiar. A function is periodic if it repeats exactly after some fixed interval.

Definition (Periodic Function - Simple Version)
A function repeats with period $T$ if shifting the input by $T$ gives the same output: $$f(x + T) = f(x) \text{ for all } x$$

Example: Sine Wave

The function $\sin(x)$ repeats every $2\pi$:

  • $\sin(0) = 0$
  • $\sin(2\pi) = 0$
  • $\sin(4\pi) = 0$

In general, $\sin(x + 2\pi) = \sin(x)$ for any $x$.

2.2 The Leap to Quasiperiodicity

Now here's the key insight: what if we add two periodic functions with incommensurable periods (periods whose ratio is irrational)?

Worked Example: Building a Quasiperiodic Function

Let's add two cosine waves:

  • Wave 1: $\cos(x)$ with period $2\pi$
  • Wave 2: $\cos(\sqrt{2}x)$ with period $2\pi/\sqrt{2}$

The combined function is: $f(x) = \cos(x) + \cos(\sqrt{2}x)$

Question: Does this combined function repeat?

Answer: No! Because $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational, there's no common period. The pattern goes on forever without exact repetition.

2.3 The Torus: A Perfect Home for Quasiperiodicity

To really understand quasiperiodic functions, we need to talk about the torus. Don't worry, we'll build up to it slowly!

Building a Torus, Step by Step

  1. Start with a square: Imagine a square piece of paper
  2. Make a cylinder: Roll it up and glue the top edge to the bottom edge
  3. Make a torus: Bend the cylinder and glue the two circular ends together

But here's the magical part: we can also think of the torus as a square where:

  • When you walk off the right edge, you appear on the left
  • When you walk off the top edge, you appear on the bottom

This is like the classic video game Pac-Man!

2.4 Geodesics: Straight Lines on a Torus

If you walk "straight" on a torus (never turning left or right), your path is called a geodesic. Here's where it gets interesting:

Interactive Figure 2: Click two points to create a geodesic. The slope determines everything:
• Rational slope (like 1/2, 2/3): The line eventually returns to its starting point: it's periodic!
• Irrational slope (like √2, π): The line never closes, eventually filling the entire torus: it's quasiperiodic!

The Mathematics Behind the Magic

When you draw a line with slope $m = \Delta y/\Delta x$ on the flat torus:

  • If $m$ is rational (like $2/3$), the geodesic closes after going around 2 times vertically and 3 times horizontally
  • If $m$ is irrational (like $\sqrt{2}$), the geodesic never closes but comes arbitrarily close to every point. This is called being dense

2.5 Connecting Geodesics to Quasiperiodic Functions

Here's the beautiful connection: walking along a geodesic on the torus is mathematically equivalent to evaluating a quasiperiodic function!

The Deep Connection

Consider a function $F(u, v)$ defined on the torus (where $u$ and $v$ are angles from 0 to $2\pi$). If we walk along a straight line with slope $\omega$, our position at time $t$ is:

  • $u(t) = t \pmod{2\pi}$
  • $v(t) = \omega t \pmod{2\pi}$

The function we trace out is: $f(t) = F(t, \omega t)$

This is exactly our definition of a quasiperiodic function when $\omega$ is irrational!

3. Quasiperiodic Functions in Multiple Variables

3.1 From Lines to Surfaces

So far, we've looked at functions of one variable, like $f(x) = \cos(x) + \cos(\sqrt{2}x)$. But the real world is three-dimensional! Let's extend our ideas.

Definition (Quasiperiodic Function in Multiple Variables)
A function $f(x, y)$ is quasiperiodic if we can write it as: $$f(x, y) = F(\omega_1 x + \omega_2 y, \omega_3 x + \omega_4 y)$$ where $F$ is periodic in both arguments, and the frequencies $\omega_1, \omega_2, \omega_3, \omega_4$ are chosen so the function never exactly repeats.

3.2 A Concrete Example: Quasiperiodic Wallpaper

Example: 2D Quasiperiodic Pattern

Let's create a "quasiperiodic wallpaper" pattern:

$$f(x, y) = \cos(x + y) + \cos(\sqrt{2}x - y) + \cos(x + \phi y)$$

where $\phi = (1 + \sqrt{5})/2$ is the golden ratio.

This function combines three wave patterns:

  1. One moving diagonally at 45°
  2. One at an irrational angle involving $\sqrt{2}$
  3. One at an angle involving the golden ratio

The result? A pattern that looks almost regular but never exactly repeats!

3.4 Understanding Winding Numbers

Winding Numbers Explained

On a 3D torus (doughnut shape), a geodesic can wind around in two ways:

  • Poloidal winding: Around the "small circle" (through the doughnut hole)
  • Toroidal winding: Around the "big circle" (around the doughnut's body)

If these winding numbers form a rational ratio, the geodesic closes. If not, it fills the surface densely!

4. Connections to Quasicrystals

4.1 From Mathematics to Matter

Remember Shechtman's "impossible" discovery? Let's understand how our mathematical framework explains real quasicrystals.

The Atomic Jigsaw Puzzle

Traditional crystals are like tiling a floor with square tiles: perfectly periodic. Quasicrystals are like Penrose tilings that fill space completely without gaps or overlaps, but never repeat!

4.2 The Cut-and-Project Method

Here's a mind-blowing way to create quasicrystals: start in higher dimensions!

Recipe for a Quasicrystal

  1. Start with a 6D cubic lattice (impossible to visualize, but mathematically simple)
  2. Choose a 3D "slice" at an irrational angle
  3. Project nearby points onto your 3D slice
  4. Result: A 3D quasicrystal!

It's like taking a diagonal slice through a regular grid. If the angle is irrational, the intersection points never repeat periodically.

4.3 Visualizing Quasicrystalline Diffraction Patterns

Important Distinction: Diffraction Patterns vs. Quasicrystals

What we're visualizing here are diffraction patterns: what you see when X-rays scatter off a quasicrystal. These wave interference patterns have the same forbidden symmetries as quasicrystals, but they're continuous functions, not discrete atomic positions.

Think of it this way: If a quasicrystal is like a Penrose tiling (discrete tiles), then what we're showing is like its shadow or Fourier transform, capturing the symmetry but not the actual structure.

5.2 Numerical Precision and Challenges

Behind the Scenes: Computational Challenges

Computers can't store irrational numbers exactly, so we use approximations. This means:

  • A "quasiperiodic" geodesic might eventually close due to rounding
  • We limit animations to prevent infinite loops
  • Real quasiperiodicity is a mathematical idealization

Despite these limitations, the visualizations capture the essential behavior!

6. Special Functions and Examples

6.1 The Triply Periodic Function

Now let's explore a specific function that appears in many physical applications:

The Canonical Triply Periodic Function

$$F(x,y,z) = \cos(x)\cos(y) + \cos(y)\cos(z) + \cos(z)\cos(x)$$

6.2 Understanding Through Level Sets

To visualize this 3D function, we look at its level sets: surfaces where $F(x,y,z) = c$ for different constants $c$.

What You're Seeing

When $c = 0$, the level set $F(x,y,z) = 0$ creates a beautiful minimal surface called the Schwarz P surface. It divides space into two intertwining labyrinths!

Figure 5: Cross-sections of the triply periodic function at different heights.

6.3 From Periodic to Quasiperiodic

To make this function quasiperiodic, we evaluate it along an irrational direction:

Creating Quasiperiodicity

Instead of $F(x,y,z)$, consider:

$$f(t) = F(t, \sqrt{2}t, \phi t)$$

where $\phi$ is the golden ratio. Now we're tracing a path through the 3D periodic structure at irrational angles, creating a 1D quasiperiodic function!

6.4 Piecewise Linear Examples: μG4 and μG7

Not all interesting functions are smooth. The μG4 and μG7 families are piecewise linear functions that arise from polyhedra.

What Are μG4 and μG7?

Imagine creating a surface from flat polygonal faces (like origami). The height function above any point gives a piecewise linear function. When we take irrational slices through these polyhedral surfaces, we get quasiperiodic piecewise linear functions.

μG4: Based on a 4-connected graph (each vertex connects to 4 edges)

μG7: Based on a 7-connected graph (each vertex connects to 7 edges)

These appear in:

  • Crystallography (atomic surface models)
  • Materials science (grain boundaries)
  • Mathematical physics (integrable systems)

7. Physical Applications

7.1 Electronic Properties of Quasiperiodic Systems

When electrons move through a quasiperiodic potential (like in a quasicrystal), strange things happen:

The Quantum Weirdness

In periodic crystals, electrons form "bands" (ranges of allowed energies. In quasiperiodic systems:

  • The spectrum can be a fractal (Cantor set)
  • Electronic states can be "critical": neither extended nor localized
  • Conductivity shows unusual temperature dependence

7.2 Magnetoresistance of Lead

Lead provides a fascinating example of quasiperiodicity in physics. When you apply a magnetic field to lead, its electrical resistance oscillates in a complex pattern.

The Lead Story

What happens: In a magnetic field, electrons in lead follow helical paths. The resistance depends on how these paths interact with the Fermi surface (the surface in momentum space separating occupied from unoccupied electron states).

The quasiperiodic connection: The Fermi surface of lead has a complex shape. When electron orbits sample this surface at different magnetic fields, they create quasiperiodic oscillations in resistance.

Why it matters: These oscillations reveal the shape of the Fermi surface, like using sound waves to map the ocean floor!

Simplified Model

The magnetoresistance oscillations can be approximated as:

$$\Delta R(B) \sim \sum_i A_i \cos\left(\frac{2\pi F_i}{B} + \phi_i\right)$$

where:

  • $B$ is the magnetic field strength
  • $F_i$ are characteristic frequencies related to extremal orbits on the Fermi surface
  • When ratios $F_i/F_j$ are irrational, the pattern is quasiperiodic!

7.3 Modern Applications

Where Quasiperiodicity Shines

Photonic Quasicrystals

  • Control light propagation in novel ways
  • Create optical devices with unique properties
  • Enable better solar cells and LEDs

Metamaterials

  • Quasiperiodic arrangements of resonators
  • Unusual acoustic or electromagnetic properties
  • Potential for cloaking devices

Twisted Bilayer Graphene

  • Two graphene layers at an irrational angle
  • Creates quasiperiodic moiré patterns
  • Exhibits superconductivity at "magic angles"
